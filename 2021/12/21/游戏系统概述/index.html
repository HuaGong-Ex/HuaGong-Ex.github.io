1.What is a game system

1.1basic

System is a unified whole composed of interconnected and interacting elements, and the basic characteristics of the system are wholeness, orderliness and optimization of internal structure.

system and elements of the relationship, in a certain sense, is the whole and part of the relationship.

The so-called ** game system **, refers to the ** game by the interaction of a number of design mechanisms, with a specific functional role of the organic whole **.

The significance of its existence is to show the game mechanism in a modular way, and play a specific function for us to understand the game, develop the game, and players to experience the game. In other words, when we make a game system, we use a series of interrelated mechanisms to realize a whole specific purpose/experience.

1.2 Narrowly defined game systems

Materialistic dialectics emphasizes the role of game systems as collections of mechanisms and wholes.

2. Composition of the game system

Generally speaking, in terms of general categorization, game systems are divided into main systems and branch systems.

We call the system** with many rules but relatively convergent and interconnected functions and contents the main system.

The systems** with fewer rules and relatively independent and single functions and contents are called subsystems or sub-systems.

In terms of a whole collection of games, all game systems can be categorized into six main systems.

All game systems can be divided into six main systems.

They are:

(1) Basic system, the system module that supports the basic operation function of the game, but does not belong to the playable content of the game. For example, account login system, game setting system, etc.

(2) Foster system, system modules of the game that include character development, pet development, equipment development and other development contents.

(3) Combat system, system modules related to the way the game is played including monster system, skill system, firearms system (shooting game), etc.

(4) Gameplay System, the gameplay in the game, such as battlefield play, arena play, copy levels, open world, etc.

(5) Economic system, system modules in the game that cover output, accumulation, trading, consumption, etc.

(6) Social system, the system module of the game that covers everyone‚Äôs interaction, human-computer interaction and other contents.‰∫§Êòì„ÄÅÊ∂àËÄóÁ≠âÁéØËäÇÁöÑÁ≥ªÁªüÊ®°Âùó„ÄÇ

The six main systems of common games

The six main systems continue to be subdivided, and there are many sub-systems.

For example, in the development system, there are pet development system, equipment development system, and other sub development system (sub-system).

That is to say, the combination of many sub-systems (also called sub-systems) becomes the main system.

The six main systems constitute the system framework of the entire game.

This is what we in the industry often refer to as the ‚Äúgame system framework‚Äù.

It is important to note that

It is important to note that the six main systems are not independent of each other, but are interconnected and complementary to each other.

This is mainly due to the fact that the sub-systems that support the main system may themselves have design content that belongs to the two main systems.

For example, the trading behavior in the economic system itself belongs to a kind of social behavior and is a part of the social system.



3. How to build a game system

3.1 Positioning

What is the positioning of this system in the whole game? Core/Extension/Auxiliary/Commercial? Nurturing/playing/socializing? How does it relate to other systems in the game? ‚ÄìTo do this you need to simply construct a game-wide system brain map that clearly outlines the positioning of this system.

Positioning is at the top of the list because it‚Äôs relevant to the construction of the entire game system - how should it be experienced? How should it be structurally connected to other systems? What should be the majority of the overall access to resources? All of this should be determined by the positioning first.

P5‚Äôs COOP system, for example, is positioned overall as a core RPG development system. This determines the weight it carries in the day-to-day, as well as its countervailing influence on the combat system in an RPG. Most importantly, this positioning gives some experiential requirements, and then the mechanics can be designed to build the system to that end (after all, as we said in part 1, a system is a collection of mechanics).


3.2 Purpose and experience

Why do we have this system? Does it make up for some kind of attribute quest? Complements some type of strength validation? Pulls in some kind of relationship building? Satisfies some experiential need? Commercializes a certain type of effect on activity/payment? Or maybe it has so many negative impacts that it drags down or even destroys the original elegant product framework?

Let‚Äôs go back to the original definition:

And by game system, I mean the organic whole of a game consisting of a number of design mechanisms that interact with each other and have a specific functional role**.

‚ÄúOrganic whole with a specific role ‚Äú is key in the definition. Any system comes into existence to build a particular play style or solve a problem in a game. I‚Äôm sure when people start out making games they build their systems completely intuitively and put in whatever they feel should be there. Of course, these intuitions should not be unsupported; especially in special cases, it‚Äôs important to think through what the system does and what kind of gameplay and experience it creates.

The 13 Sentinels: Aegis Rim‚Äôs Thinking Cloud is an information gathering and interaction system. It serves a very direct and obvious purpose - to store player-acquired information for on-the-fly replay and hints; or to use information/props to interact with scene characters and advance the plot. The galgame equivalent of integrated replay and advancement is the Item Bar of information. This is an excellent system design in a narrative-based, information-heavy game.


3.3 Structure

Break out the internal structure of the system, the relationship of the subsystems within the system to each other in terms of correlations or influences, the relationship of resource flow within the system, and the system play of the system in terms of its inputs and outputs and the relative connection of inputs and outputs to the outside world.

‚Äì Like a circuit diagram or a mechanical structure diagram, a diagram to see clearly the internal structure and operation rules.

This is a breakdown of the Hollow Knight Dependency Stack that I did a long time ago, and I can see that one must have a clear idea of the game system structure in order to at least know what to do first, what to do second, and how and how much it relates to other systems. Following this stack, I found that one characteristic of this type of Metroidvania is that character growth comes first, i.e., the acquisition of character abilities must be designed before the design of specific maps, as it relates to very specific process control.

Another interesting point is that in this system design of Hollow Knight, character growth and development are revealed by the props bar, i.e., growth and props are linked, and whether or not it needs to be made this way should also be determined early on.


3.4 Resource Placement

  1. What resources/data are involved in this system?

  2. What behaviors can a player perform in this system?

  3. How does player behavior lead to a flow of resources? How does the flow of resources in turn affect player behavior?

In long-running games, the system often needs to take on the function of moving resources in and out. How to balance resources (economy, combat, etc.) so that the overall resource system doesn‚Äôt collapse is something that must be thought about before designing the system, which often seems more complicated in MMORPGs. Other games also need to think about this often in terms of their economic systems, take Hearthstone Legends‚Äô combat passes for example, what resources do the passes provide? How much does it provide? What is the cap? How fast do players acquire them? How does it promote charging and balancing? All of these have to be viewed in the context of positioning and purpose.

In fact, besides the economic system, other systems and resource placement are also closely related. Besides, in the battle system of Hearthstone Legends, mana crystal is a resource, life value is a resource, follower is also a resource, and armor is also a resource ‚Ä¶‚Ä¶ There can be many different kinds of resources, and each of them requires input to be considered.


3.5 Overall Interaction and Conceptual Packaging

How is the interaction layout of the system? Where is the main focus, where are the information areas, where are the manipulation areas, and is the interaction layout harmonized with other systems?

How is the interaction path of the system? Are the action lines smooth and consistent with visual and operational habits? Are the click steps as few and easy as possible? Is the interaction path of the front and back interfaces coherent without any sense of separation?

How is the physicality of the system‚Äôs interaction? Is the size of the buttons comfortable to click, is the sensitivity of dragging and sliding appropriate, and does the sound, color, and form of the clicked control produce a pleasant sensation?

How is the system‚Äôs interactive assistance? Is the information text clear and easy to read, is the graphical information easy to recognize without ambiguity, is the visual highlighting of buttons and text easy to grasp the key points or assist in decision making, and is the explanation of the input and output prompts sufficient?

System interaction is actually something that can be considered last, and it can also be placed alongside conceptual packaging and the like as a means to assist in creating the desired experience. It‚Äôs important, but it doesn‚Äôt prevent us from considering it later, because it doesn‚Äôt touch the bottom of the design.

The pathfinding mechanic in Ghost of Tsushima‚Äôs exploration system interacts in an extremely elegant way - the touchpad triggers the winds of guidance, maximizing and simplifying the UI - and I just finished Assassin‚Äôs Creed: Odyssey with a spring in my step, so that instead of being distracted by eye-catching icons, I can focus on enjoying the scenery, riding my horse, and catching up on my travels.

Similar interactions - guided by the Golden Wind - will be used in The Elden Ring, and we can expect to see more use of this type of mechanic in future open-world games.



After understanding this, it‚Äôs time to disassemble or design your own systems along these lines. Full-stack planners in indie game teams often have to work independently on the architecture of the entire game system, and this is a great opportunity to build your own system stack and deepen your understanding of it. Because game systems are often interconnected and intertwined, we need to double-check our system design, or else we‚Äôll miss a lot of connections - because at the end of the day, no matter what, the system is just an organic component of the overall product.
