One word on Celeste‚Äôs moving experience: excellent feel (control), character dexterity, energizing feedback, and excellent level pacing.

1. Analyze moving features

1.1 Resource Management

Puzzle solving based on resource management is a major feature of Celeste.

In the early stage, resources consist of fixed climbing energy + two jump opportunities + level resources (such as green crystals to replenish jump opportunities).

Players need to manage their own resources and collect map resources to plan the path through the level.

Such resource management style puzzles are not the only ones to solve, and their level design fits this mobile feature well, usually with more path options.

The subsequent addition of triple jumps actually adds fixed resources globally, providing the basis for complex level building.

1.2 Inclusivity

Inclusivity is mainly reflected in some detailed mechanics, such as: jump pre-input; edge correction; and non-strict maneuvers (stomping wall jumps).

Pressing jump in advance before landing will result in a jump at the moment of landing. (Pre-input)

Stomp wall jumps don‚Äôt have to be against a wall, only 2 pixels away. (Non-strict input)

Sprinting will avoid obstacles and automatically correct your position. (Edge correction)

These mechanics are extremely inclusive, and while they may not seem strict, they optimize the feel of the player and add smoothness to the experience. Some of these mechanisms will be mentioned specifically later.

1.3 Excellent level pacing

Celeste‚Äôs levels are relatively short, and the ability to dynamically control the pacing of the levels using the existing movement mechanics within this very small level space is what makes it so special.

The control of the level‚Äôs pacing is mainly due to the placement of sprint point refills (green diamonds, jelly walls, bubbles) and the temporary safety zones created by the climbing mechanic (a cushion halfway through a move before landing). The playing experience is: stop and plan a route for a fast-paced displacement, which can also be flexibly controlled according to the player‚Äôs level and proficiency in the buffer time. Then stop again to cycle into a route planning stage, the overall slack.

2.2Motion Frames Design Pattern

By disassembling the specific frame rates for running, jumping, and sprinting, you can get a glimpse of the design model behind it. The platforming jumps were not designed based on the engine‚Äôs own physics system, and little attention was paid to the realism of the character‚Äôs movements. Instead, they were designed to feel right in the hands of the player, and the parameters of the movements were developed and debugged (5,471 lines of script were used to control the Player).

When disassembling the game, we can clearly divide the movement into a series of ‚Äústages‚Äù, i.e. a simple movement is composed of several ‚Äústage animations‚Äù. This design facilitates flexible adjustment during development, and provides the player with an upper limit of fine-tuning to find the key nodes of the ‚Äústages‚Äù within a single action, making it highly playable.


3. Extended play

3.1 Running jump

3.2 Jump Pre-Input

Jump pre-input realizes the seamlessness of the action, allowing the player to perform a continuous landing jump (usually accompanied by a large displacement of the x-axis), without strictly requiring the player to land on the ground, which ensures a coherent and smooth feel; with inclusiveness, the handicapped player can also experience the joy of the high-speed-moving .

3.3 Kick Wall Jump

This mechanic is scene dependent and does not need to be strictly against a wall (<2 pixels is fine) to be inclusive. It creates a smooth, fluid, lightweight feel.

3.4 Wolf Jump

Wolf jumping refers to the ability to jump a few frames off the ground.

Understandably, it is a mechanism designed to be inclusive of the player‚Äôs maneuvers, and this has spawned a higher upper limit of maneuvers for the speedpass player to utilize (even extending to being jumped on by a jab).

3.5 Climbing behavior

You can perform jumping and sprinting behaviors while climbing.

3.6 Sprint Interrupt

In any situation (on the ground, in the air, on a wall, and sometimes when a sprint is in progress), a sprint can be prioritized and judged to be performed as long as there is a sprint point.

Once a sprint is input, it will essentially execute the move, but it is possible to interrupt a sprint near the end (~5 pixels away) to perform the next sprint. The player basically feels no noticeable interruption, and this is similar to the pre-input before a jump landing.

3.7 Sprint Correction

This is a mechanic based on sprinting: when a sprint encounters an obstacle, if the obstacle is not too large, it can be automatically corrected to a sprint across the obstacle.

3.8 Momentum Storage

Cranes, clouds, and walls of jelly all have some kind of drive effect.

3.9 Big Jumps, Lingering Steps

Similar to the Crane‚Äôs momentum storage, Madeline can utilize the inertia of her running and jumping, or leverage the force of her jumps to achieve extra-long jumps. This type of maneuver can be implemented in a ‚Äústage‚Äù design pattern that depends on the frame rate of the maneuver, as mentioned in the previous section.

The most common dive: jump + dash downward diagonally, then quickly jump to the right.

It can also evolve into a Super Ultra Dash by utilizing high and low terrain.


4. Design Summary

4.1 Design Concept

4.1.1 Coherent Design

Coherent design involves a number of operational details. In Celeste, this is mainly reflected in the jump pre-input (2 pixels) and dash interrupt (5 pixels). Characteristics: small decision intervals; decisions are made before the end of the action; the next action is performed immediately and continuously. Design Purpose: The coherence design is clearly intended to ensure the smoothness of the game, and to create a smooth feel in the hand. Because in pixel-level games, it is virtually impossible for the player to catch the exact frame of the landing to jump, a short pre-input decision is required to realize a continuous jump. Sprints are not pre-inputted, but rather interrupted, as the new sprint is the highest priority in any given situation, and this balance must be maintained. The timing of the pre-input/interrupt is so short that the actual player experience is hard to notice, ensuring that ‚Äúthe character is still in the player‚Äôs control‚Äù, but with a more consistent feel.

4.1.2 Inclusive design and operational caps

Inclusive design is similar to coherent design in that it allows a certain degree of latitude in judgment. Coherent design emphasizes the continuity of movement articulation, while inclusive design emphasizes the tolerance of individual movement judgments. Wolf Jump (10 frames), Wall Stomp (2 pixels), and Dash Compensation are all inclusive designs, and these designs in fact bring a higher upper limit. Characteristics: Smaller judgment interval; judgment is made during the effective interval of the action. Design Purpose: Increasing the judgment range also helps the player to realize an action more easily, and it raises the lower and upper limits of the action while keeping the feeling of consistency and smoothness. For more advanced techniques, such as the Super Wall Stomp (Upward Sprint Wall Stomp), the decision interval for the Wall Stomp will be larger (about 5 pixels), which encourages the player to attempt more precise and rigorous maneuvers to improve their skill level.

4.2 Primary Design Ideas

Priority: As a level-based platform jumping game, Celeste is designed so that the main characters can move without any problems, and the feel of the game (control and smoothness) is good, and then it introduces a variety of auxiliary movement mechanisms in different levels with different flavors.

Player Orientation: The movement mechanism of Celeste is mainly targeted at three categories of people - handicapped players, or players who just want to experience the story (Class A); players who have the pursuit of operation, but with average skills (Class B); and players who have advanced achievements in speed pass/multi-faceted (Class C).

For Class A and some Class B players, Celeste will be a difficult game, and the highest priority in the design was to make the entire movement mechanics simple and easy to understand, and to improve the judgment and lower limit of the operation. For this reason, less basic movement and inclusive design is introduced, and the lag time of jumping and sprinting curves actually facilitates players to adjust their landing points and lower the threshold of operation. (The care for AB players is also reflected in the low cost of death: short levels, quick resurrection, etc.)

For class C players, it is indispensable to create an operation ceiling, in addition to the slightly increased inclusiveness design, it is also necessary to design a reasonable set of high-level gameplay to test the operation, which introduces the combination mechanism of kinetic energy storage (High-speed-moving, big jump, no stamina on the wall, reverse jumping, etc.), this set of action combinations to provide the player with a very high operation ceiling, and supporting the introduction of high-level levels. For B players, the designers wanted them to be able to learn advanced maneuvers as well. This introduced a greater degree of judgment latitude for higher-level maneuvers, as well as explicit teaching of higher-level maneuvers in Chapter 9 to make these seemingly extreme maneuvers easier to learn and implement.

4.3 Level Pace Oriented

Rhythm-oriented: As a level-based game, in addition to designing a series of basic movement mechanics for the three types of players, Celeste‚Äôs movement mechanics must also serve the construction of the level and control the rhythm.

In a level, players will sometimes need to perform continuous maneuvers in the air, and at other times they will need to stop in safe zones to provide time for reflection and buffering. This is reflected in Celeste‚Äôs basic movement mechanics of sprinting and climbing.

In order to create a smooth experience of continuous maneuvering in the air, it was necessary to start with both character movement mechanics and level movement mechanics. The first step is to introduce a series of props (springs, cranes, clouds, etc.) to assist the player‚Äôs displacement in the level, and then design a mechanism for expanding the character‚Äôs mobility, which introduces the design of sprinting and sprinting points, as well as a series of collectible props to replenish sprinting points. In this way, the character‚Äôs ability to move continuously is linked to the level design.

Finally, the player‚Äôs continuous movement needs to be buffered by a safety zone for a limited time in order to control the pacing of the level to follow the player‚Äôs maneuvering level. This introduces a climbing mechanic (in this case, clinging to walls) that creates a brief lull in movement.
